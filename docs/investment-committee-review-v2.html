1Executive Summary

This is the board's second review. The first review (June 2026) concluded 🟠 Significant Modelling Improvements Required, driven by four P0 defects: a growth-timing bias that flattered every later purchase, a goal engine that maximised instead of solved, plans that ran cash down to £1,246, and no lender affordability test. On re-examination, every P0 and P1 item from that review has been remediated, and verified in the live engine — plus two of the three P2 items. This is the rare remediation programme that actually shipped.

The engine now models, correctly and verifiably: per-property acquisition-dated growth; a goal solver that stops at the target (+5% margin); a configurable cash reserve enforced during generation; a lender ICR stress test (pay-rate +2% / 5.5% floor) as both a buy gate and a KPI; fixed-rate expiry and repricing; mortgage arrangement, valuation and legal fees; early repayment charges on every early exit — including strategy-generated ones; lumpy capex on a per-property cycle; rent arrears distinct from voids; post-tax cashflow under S24 or corporation tax; CGT on disposal; and a professional returns block (equity multiple, ROCE, cash-on-cash, net yield on cost, payback, debt-maturity calendar). The strategy lineup has been rebuilt into a genuine four-point efficient frontier, including a true equity-release BRRR strategy, and the previously mislabelled "Mortgage Recycler" has been honestly renamed "Low-Risk Hold".

Running the four strategies live against the stored £5k/month goal (15-year horizon, Ltd structure, 60% LTV mandate, £15k starting cash, £15k/yr director loans), the board found the engine's own guardrails working as designed: only Maximise Cashflow reaches the goal (month 143, £7,267/month post-tax); BRRR generated a plan that breaches the 60% LTV mandate (peaking at 66.8%) and the engine correctly flagged it infeasible and declined to recommend it. That is exactly what a committee wants to see — a system that tells the truth about its own outputs.

What still stops full sign-off

Five findings, none structural: (1) the candidate deal is frozen in nominal terms — every purchase across 15 years costs £150,000 and rents at £1,200, when the engine's own 3%/2.5% growth assumptions imply ~£228,000 and ~£1,724 by year 14, systematically overstating how many units are affordable late in the plan; (2) the BRRR generator sizes refinances to 75% LTV without consulting the goal's own 60% mandate — it generates plans its own constraint-checker must then reject; (3) the IRR solver returns no value on all four generated strategies, leaving the flagship return metric blank precisely where it matters; (4) a min_icr of 0 on the goal silently disables the lender gate rather than falling back to the 125/145% default; (5) the projection remains a single deterministic path — no downturn case, no probability band, no concentration warning (first-review item #10, still open).

None of these corrupts the cash accounting — they are enumerable, parameter-level defects with clear fixes. The board's overall finding is that the engine has crossed the line from "credible architecture, unreliable numbers" to "reliable numbers, with known and bounded blind spots."

3Review Methodology

The board did not rely on stored outputs or the previous report's figures. All four strategies were regenerated live against the current codebase and the goal's stored configuration, and the engine source was re-inspected line-by-line for each remediated item.

Evidence base

  • Live generation runs of all four pathways against the real database (3-property Falkirk-area starting portfolio: two unencumbered flats at £60k, one £80k house with £48k fixed-rate debt).
  • Stored goal configuration: £5,000/month income, Ltd structure, max LTV 60%, 3-month reserve, £15,000 starting cash, £15,000/yr director loans from June 2027, 5-year refix at +100bps, 3% ERC.
  • Stored global assumptions: 25% deposit, 5.5% rate, £1,500 legal, £0 arrangement/valuation fee (operator-weakened — see §9), 3% growth, 2.5% rent growth, 2.5% cost inflation, 1 void month/yr, 0.5% arrears, £3k capex/10yr.
  • Code inspection of the projection engine, pathway generator, tax module and return-metrics module, including the full test suite (262 passing).

Candidate deal under test

  • £150,000 purchase, £1,200/month rent (9.6% gross yield), 25% deposit, 5.5% over 25 years.
  • Projection horizon 15 years; all four templates generated from identical inputs.
  • The board verified event streams (buys, payoffs, cash-out remortgages), month-series cash positions, and the returns block per strategy.
  • Where a number looked wrong, the board traced it to source before attributing fault (e.g. the IRR blanks and BRRR's LTV breach, §6).

5Detailed Strategy Analysis

5.1 Maximise Cashflow  Risk: Medium-High  Engine's recommendation

Strengths

  • The only strategy that reaches the goal — £7,267/month post-tax by month 143, a 45% overshoot that provides genuine margin.
  • Interest-only financing maximises per-unit cashflow while ICR never breaches (min 199% vs 145% Ltd floor) and LTV peaks at 58%, inside the 60% mandate.
  • Best cash-on-cash among the growth strategies (11.5%).

Weaknesses

  • £1.6m of never-amortising debt refixing every 5 years — the plan's viability is a leveraged bet on the rate path, and the engine's +100bps refix assumption is operator-softened (§9).
  • Worst equity multiple of the feasible strategies (2.64×): income is bought with balance-sheet risk, not created efficiently.
  • No exit story: at year 15 the £1.6m is still owed, with no modelled plan for redemption or term-end refinancing beyond the horizon.

Board view: a legitimate professional strategy, correctly risk-scored (57 vs ~0 for the amortising plans) and correctly flagged "interest-only — rate-exposed". The committee accepts the engine's recommendation logic (it is the only goal-reacher) but notes the recommendation banner should carry the risk trade-off more loudly — the safest feasible plan and the recommended plan are at opposite ends of the risk table.

5.2 Target & Hold  Risk: Medium

Ten repayment purchases, then holds. Ends at £3,504/month post-tax — it does not reach the £5k goal within 15 years, and the engine says so plainly, attributing the constraint honestly: "limited by cash reserve — dipped to £6,738 vs a £6,600 floor." This is the correct behaviour the first review demanded (a solver that respects constraints rather than fabricating success), but it exposes a labelling problem: a strategy named "Target & Hold" that cannot hit the target reads as a broken promise rather than an honest constraint report. The board recommends the UI surface "goal not reachable under these constraints — binding: reserve" as the card's headline, not a footnote. Respectable middle-of-the-road numbers otherwise (3.15×, 14.3% ROCE, 52% peak LTV).

5.3 Low-Risk Hold  Risk: Low

Strengths

  • The capital-efficiency champion: 4.58× equity multiple, 23.9% ROCE, 20.1% cash-on-cash on just £287k invested — roughly half the capital of Target & Hold for 84% of its ending income.
  • Fortress balance sheet: 43.7% peak LTV falling to £218k final debt across 8 properties; min ICR 299%.
  • The honest rename (from "Mortgage Recycler") resolves the first review's credibility flag exactly as recommended.

Weaknesses

  • Falls £207/month short of the goal (£4,793 vs £5,000) — tantalisingly close; a marginally larger candidate deal or one more unit likely clears it, but the engine offers no "nearest feasible fix" hint.
  • Four early payoffs incur 3% ERCs — the engine now correctly charges these (first review item #6), and the strategy remains attractive even after them.
  • 40-month payback is the longest of the four.

Board view: the plan most members would run with their own money. Its risk-adjusted dominance is now visible because the returns block exists — exactly why the first review demanded those metrics.

5.4 BRRR  Generated infeasible

The new equity-release strategy executes its brief with enthusiasm — 18 purchases funded by 31 cash-out refinances, building the largest portfolio (21 units, £3.48m) — but refinances each property to 75% LTV without consulting the goal's 60% portfolio mandate, peaking at 66.8%. The engine's feasibility checker catches the breach, marks the plan infeasible, and the ranking correctly declines to recommend it. The guardrails work; the generator does not. A strategy generator that produces plans its own compliance layer must reject is wasted compute and — worse — presents an investor with a tantalising 21-property plan stamped "infeasible" rather than the best BRRR plan that fits the mandate (refinance target ≈ min(75%, goal ceiling)). The returns block also quietly indicts the strategy as configured: 2.11× and 7.4% ROCE — all that churn, fees and ERC for the worst capital efficiency of the four. BRRR economics depend on below-market purchases and forced appreciation (the "refurbish" step), which the engine does not yet model — without a refurb-uplift input, BRRR is recycling fees, not value.

6Financial Logic & Internal Consistency

The first review's three material accounting findings are all fixed and verified. The board traced this review's numbers to source and found the cash engine internally consistent — money is never spent twice, capital events reconcile, tax follows structure. Three new findings, in descending severity:

6.1 The candidate deal is frozen in nominal terms High

Every purchase across the 15-year horizon costs exactly £150,000 and rents at exactly £1,200/month — the year-14 acquisition is priced identically to the year-1 acquisition. Under the engine's own assumptions (3% property growth, 2.5% rent growth), that year-14 deal should cost ≈ £228,000 with ≈ £1,724/month rent. The two errors do not cancel: the understated price cuts the deposit-plus-costs hurdle by ~35%, so late purchases happen years earlier than real cash could support, inflating unit counts and ending income on every multi-purchase strategy. This is the same class of optimism bias as the growth-timing defect the first review rated Critical — it survives because it lives in the deal assumption, not the projection. It is rated High rather than Critical only because the direction and rough magnitude are obvious to a professional user, and it affects plan speed rather than accounting truth. Fix: index the candidate price/rent to the growth assumptions at each purchase month.

6.2 BRRR ignores the goal's LTV mandate when sizing refinances High

The generator refinances to a hard-coded 75% target while the goal specifies a 60% maximum. Generation and compliance disagree about the rules of the game; compliance wins, and the investor receives a rejected plan instead of the best compliant one. Fix: cap the refinance target at the goal's max_ltv_pct (with the buy-gate given the same awareness).

6.3 The IRR solver returns no value on every generated strategy Medium

All four plans display "—" for IRR. The board traced the cause: the bisection solver requires a sign change in NPV across its search band, but a portfolio that starts cash-generative and is drip-funded by director loans produces a cashflow series whose NPV stays positive across the entire bracket — there is no root to find, and the solver honestly returns nothing rather than a fabricated number. Honest, but not useful: the flagship professional metric is blank precisely on realistic inputs. Fix: compute IRR on the investor's incremental flows (capital contributed vs. distributions + terminal equity), or fall back to a modified-IRR / annualised-multiple when no root exists — with a tooltip explaining which was used. The other five return metrics compute correctly and reconcile with the raw series.

The min_icr = 0 footgun

The goal stores min_icr = 0, and because the fallback logic only substitutes the tax-derived 125/145% default when the field is null, a zero passes straight through — every candidate deal "passes" a 0% ICR floor and the lender gate is effectively off for this goal. The board suspects this was an accidental entry rather than intent (the UI accepts 0 without warning). Whatever the intent, a safety system that can be switched off by typing 0, silently, is a validation defect: 0 should mean "use default", or the form should refuse values below 100 with an explicit "disable gate" checkbox. The same pattern applies to the zeroed fees and capex reserve — the Settings page now has tooltips, but no prudence guardrails.

Would a lender accept these assumptions? The stress methodology, yes — it mirrors PRA-era BTL underwriting. The configured arrears/fees/uplift values, no — an underwriter would restate them to defaults and re-run. Would an experienced investor rely on them? With the defaults restored and §6.1 fixed: yes, as a central case — which is precisely what could not be said at first review.

12Scenario-Engine Design Review

The architecture the first review praised — a pure, deterministic, event-driven month simulator with a true cash account — has scaled cleanly through the remediation. The 262-test suite, three-tier assumption fallback (event → global settings → engine default), and self-describing assumptions_json snapshots on every generated scenario are professional-grade engineering. What remains:

Missing event types

  • Refurb / onboarding-void period per acquisition (blocks honest BRRR).
  • Purchase-with-delay — offer-to-completion lag.
  • Portfolio-level product switch (rate-shopping at refix rather than passive +uplift).

Missing calculations

  • IRR fallback method for no-root cashflow shapes (§6.3).
  • Deal price/rent indexation over the horizon (§6.1).
  • s455 / DLA balance tracking; SDLT/LTT band tables + jurisdiction flag.
  • Valuation-haircut parameter on refinance events.

Missing reports & KPIs

  • Downturn case (negative growth + void spike + rate shock) as a standing companion output — the manual stress toggle exists but must be invoked.
  • Monte-Carlo band (the pure, ~ms-per-run projection function makes 500 runs trivially cheap — the architecture is ready, the feature is absent).
  • Concentration dashboard (postcode / town / type exposure — the data exists in the properties table, unaggregated).
  • "Months to insolvency under shock" — reserve adequacy as a survival metric.

Missing validations & warnings

  • min_icr = 0 (and £0 fees, £0 capex reserve) accepted silently — prudence guardrails on Settings/Goal forms (§9).
  • Generator/mandate coherence check: refuse to emit events that the feasibility layer will reject (§6.2).
  • A "configuration prudence" banner when operator values sit outside industry ranges.

Since the first review, the engine has gained: the ICR stress KPI and buy gate, scheduled repricing, full acquisition/exit cost modelling, capex and arrears, the six-metric returns block, the debt-maturity calendar, and per-property month series feeding LTV-aware strategy logic. Each was verified live. The list above is genuinely a "remaining work" list, not a re-litigation.

Could it produce better strategies?—Yes — three specific upgrades: (a) fix BRRR's mandate-blindness (§6.2); (b) a "nearest-feasible" hint when a plan just misses goal; (c) hybrid templates (e.g. IO early, switch to repayment at goal) which the event engine can already simulate but the generator never proposes.

Three of four strategies score 0, one scores 57. Directionally right, but a 0 reads as "riskless" — no leveraged property plan is. The score needs a floor, a scale (0–100 with labelled bands), or replacement by the risk-matrix ratings in §11.

AAppendix A — Downturn Standing Output & Monte-Carlo Band

Design note for roadmap item 5, jointly authored by the board's Financial Modelling Expert and Principal Software Architect. The governing insight: the hard architectural work is already done. buildProjection(initialState, events, config) is a pure, DB-free function costing ~1–5ms per 15-year run, and every assumption it consumes already arrives through one injectable surface (assumptions_json → global settings → engine literal). Both features are therefore callers of the existing engine, not changes to it — the projection loop should not gain a single line.

A.1 The downturn standing case (deterministic companion)

Modelling design. A named, fixed parameter transformation applied to whatever the central case uses — not a second set of user knobs. Recommended UK-calibrated definition, applied for the first 24 months of the projection with recovery thereafter:

ParameterCentral (as configured)Downturn transformRationale
Property growth3.0%−8% yrs 1–2, then centralPeak-to-trough of ~16% matches 1990–92 and 2008–09 UK corrections.
Rent growth2.5%0% yrs 1–2, then centralNominal rents flatline rather than fall in UK downturns.
Void1 mo/yr2 mo/yr throughoutTenant demand weakens; re-lets slow.
Arrears0.5%3% throughoutArrears are the recession variable landlords actually feel.
Refix uplift+100bps+300bps at next refixRate-spike-into-recession is the pattern that kills leveraged plans.
 The transform is versioned engine policy (a named constant set, e.g. DOWNTURN_2026_V1), not user configuration — so every plan is stressed identically and results are comparable across time.

Architecture. The two-year-then-recover growth shape is the only capability the engine lacks — assumptions are currently flat for the horizon. Two options: (a) a piecewise schedule in assumptions_json ({growth_schedule: [{months: 24, pct: -8}, {pct: 3}]}), parsed where flat values parse today; or (b) approximate with existing machinery (rate_shock_bps + a flat lower growth). The board recommends (a) — ~20 lines in the assumptions block, reusable later for user-defined rate paths. Execution: runScenario() and generatePathways() each run twice (central + transformed config), returning results_downturn alongside results. Persist both in scenario_results. Surface as: a second dashed line on the projection chart, a "Downturn" column beside every summary KPI, and — the number a committee reads first — "months of reserve survival under downturn" (first month the stressed cash line breaches the reserve floor, or "survives horizon").

A.2 The Monte-Carlo band (stochastic companion)

Modelling design — what to randomise, and what not to. The single most important decision is the sampling unit. Do not sample month-by-month i.i.d. noise: monthly independence diversifies away exactly the persistent macro risk the band exists to show, producing seductively narrow fans. Property risk is regime risk. Sample once per run (persistent parameters for that simulated world):

ParameterDistributionSuggested calibration
Property growth (annual)Normal, truncated ±3σμ = configured 3.0%, σ = 3.5%
Rent growth (annual)Normal, correlated with growthμ = 2.5%, σ = 1.5%, ρ ≈ 0.6
Void (months/yr)Shifted lognormalmedian = configured, capped at 4
Refix uplift (bps)Discrete {0, +100, +200, +300}p = {0.2, 0.4, 0.3, 0.1}
Arrears (%)Shifted lognormalmedian = configured, tail to 4%
 Growth/rent correlation via a 2-variable Cholesky (one line of arithmetic — no library). Calibrations are the modelling expert's UK BTL priors; they belong in a versioned constants file with citations, reviewed annually.

Architecture. A new pure module server/services/monteCarlo.ts, mirroring returnMetrics.ts in shape:

runMonteCarlo(initialState, events, config, { runs = 500, seed })
  → for each run: sample params → override assumptions_json → buildProjection(...)
  → collect per-month total_equity + cumulative_cashflow_posttax + ending CF
  → reduce to bands: P10 / P25 / P50 / P75 / P90 per month
  → headline: goal_probability = share of runs with ending post-tax CF ≥ target
  • Determinism is non-negotiable. A seeded PRNG (e.g. mulberry32, ~10 lines, no dependency) with the seed persisted alongside results — identical inputs must reproduce identical bands, or the feature is untestable and the committee cannot audit it.
  • Performance: 500 runs × ~2ms ≈ 1s server-side — acceptable as an on-demand POST /scenarios/:id/monte-carlo, cached in scenario_results keyed by (inputs hash, seed, runs). Do not run inside pathway generation (the greedy generator already re-projects dozens of times); bands are computed once per finished plan.
  • Presentation: a fan chart (P10–P90 shaded, P25–P75 darker, P50 line) via a range-area variant of the existing ScenarioAreaChart; the central deterministic case plotted over it as a sanity check (it should track P50 — persistent divergence means the distributions are mis-centred). Headline chip: "Reaches £5,000/month in 68% of 500 simulated worlds."
  • What the fan must not claim: label as "model uncertainty under stated distributions", never "probability of outcome" — the distributions are priors, not market data. The P50 is not "expected"; the goal-probability is conditional on the calibration file. One honest sentence in the UI footer preserves the committee's credibility standard.

Sequencing. Ship A.1 first (two runs, zero new maths, immediate committee value), then A.2 — the downturn case doubles as a fixed integration test for the piecewise-schedule machinery A.2 also needs. Combined estimate: the engine-side work is small (~200 lines + tests); the chart work dominates.

BAppendix B — Risk-Adjusted Ranking View

Design note for roadmap item 8. The current ranking is honest but one-dimensional: feasible plans sort by time-to-goal, so the recommended plan is always the fastest feasible one — which this review showed is also the riskiest feasible one. The fix is not a cleverer single number; it is making the risk/speed trade-off a first-class, user-owned choice.

B.1 First, make the risk score mean something

The prerequisite. Today's score (0, 57, 0, 0 across the four strategies) is an unbounded penalty accumulator — directionally useful, presentationally broken: a leveraged 13-property plan scoring 0 reads as "riskless". Replace it with a bounded composite, computed from summary fields that all already exist:

B.2 The ranking: explicit appetite, not a hidden formula

Modelling position. The board explicitly rejects silently collapsing risk and speed into one opaque utility number. Under Consumer Duty an adviser must be able to show which preference produced the recommendation; an owner-operator deserves the same. The design is therefore a visible ranking mode, persisted per goal:

The frontier view. Alongside the ranked cards, a small scatter — risk score (x) vs months-to-goal or ending post-tax income (y), one dot per strategy, Pareto-efficient set highlighted, infeasible plans greyed with their binding constraint. On this review's data the chart tells the whole story in one glance: Low-Risk Hold and Maximise Cashflow are both efficient (neither dominates the other), Target & Hold is dominated, BRRR is greyed. This is the artefact the committee itself reached for in §4 — the engine should draw it.

Architecture: rankPathways(rows, mode) stays pure (mode as a trailing optional param defaulting to 'fastest' — zero behaviour change until opted in); goals.ranking_mode column + a three-button toggle on the pathways panel; the recommended badge annotated with its provenance ("Recommended — safest plan reaching goal"). The frontier scatter reuses the existing Recharts wrapper layer. Tests: synthetic pathway sets where each mode provably recommends a different plan, and the BRRR-breach case pinning the "never recommend infeasible" invariant across all modes.

CAppendix C — IRR Fallback Methodology

The experts add a third appendix on their own initiative: roadmap item 4 looks like a bug fix but is actually a methodology decision, and shipping the wrong convention would put a defensible-looking but meaningless number on every plan — worse than the current honest blank.

C.1 Why the IRR is blank (precise diagnosis)

The current solver differences the portfolio's cumulative_cashflow series to recover monthly net flows, appends terminal equity, and bisects for the rate where NPV = 0. That construction measures the portfolio cash account, not the investor. Two consequences on realistic inputs: director-loan injections appear as positive flows (cash arrives in the account) when to the investor they are contributions (money leaving their pocket); and a portfolio that starts cash-generative never posts the early negative flow an IRR needs. Result: NPV > 0 at every rate in the bracket, no sign change, no root — the solver honestly returns nothing, on precisely the configurations real users have.

C.2 Recommended method: capital-account (investor-perspective) IRR

Rebuild the flow series from the investor's chair rather than differencing the cash line:

investor_flow[i] = − capital_deployed[i]        // deposits + fees + capex + ERC (already tracked
                                                //  for total_capital_invested — reuse those hooks)
                   − director_loan_in[i]        // contributions, sign-corrected
                   + director_loan_repay[i]
investor_flow[last] += end_equity + ending_cash_pot   // as-if-liquidated terminal value

Retained operating cashflow is deliberately not distributed monthly — it accumulates inside the terminal value, which mirrors how a Ltd BTL structure actually works (profits retained, extracted later) and guarantees the sign structure an IRR requires: contributions early and negative, one large terminal inflow. A root now exists by construction whenever any capital was deployed. Label it "IRR (capital-account basis)" with a tooltip stating the convention; an unlabelled IRR invites comparison against ungrounded numbers from other tools.

C.3 Fallback ladder and guardrails

TierMethodWhen
1Capital-account IRR (above)Default — expected to resolve all four current blanks.
2MIRR: finance rate = configured mortgage rate, reinvestment rate = 0Multiple sign changes (heavy BRRR churn can produce them); MIRR is single-valued by construction.
3Annualised equity multiple: (multiple)^(1/years) − 1, labelled as suchDegenerate series (no capital deployed). Never render a dash when the multiple exists.
  • Cross-check invariant (the modelling expert's acceptance test): for a synthetic single-purchase, no-loan scenario, capital-account IRR must equal the existing solver's answer to within rounding — the two constructions coincide when there are no contributions mid-stream. Add this as a unit test before switching the default.
  • Consistency invariant: IRR must always be reconcilable with the equity multiple ((1+IRR)^years ≈ multiple when flows are front-loaded); a plan showing 2.1× and 19% IRR over 15 years is arithmetically impossible and should fail a sanity assertion rather than render.
  • Architecture: one new pure function buildInvestorFlows(months, events, summary) in returnMetrics.ts; solveIRR unchanged; the tier used returned alongside the value ({ irr_pct, irr_basis: 'capital_account' | 'mirr' | 'annualised_multiple' }) so the UI can label honestly. The engine needs one small addition: per-month capital-deployed and loan-flow arrays (the scalar totals already exist; the timeline does not).

Why this earns an appendix

Items 1–3 of the roadmap are unambiguous once stated. This one is not: three IRR conventions produce three different numbers from identical plans, all "correct" under their own definition. The committee's requirement is not a number — it is a stated basis, a fallback ladder, and two invariant tests, so that whichever figure renders can survive the next review's audit.
