1Executive Summary

The engine is materially better than the spreadsheets most landlords use — it models a true cash account, draws deposits and transaction taxes (LBTT + 8% ADS) from that account, amortises debt month-by-month, and now layers post-tax cashflow (S24 / corporation tax) on top. That foundation is genuinely creditable. However, in its current state the committee would not allow it to drive a six-figure decision.

Three issues move it from "useful indicator" to "not yet decision-grade":

  • An optimism bias baked into the projection. Capital growth, rent growth and cost inflation are all measured from the portfolio start date, not each property's purchase date. A property bought in year 9 is instantly marked up ~25–30% in value and ~20% in rent the moment it is acquired. Because the recommended strategies buy 27 properties over 15 years, this systematically overstates equity and income — most for the acquisition-heavy plans.
  • The goal engine maximises acquisition rather than solving the goal. The target is £5,000/month. The "Steady" and "Accelerated" plans keep buying to the horizon and finish at 30 properties, £4.09m of assets, £1.62m of debt and £13,412/month — overshooting the goal by ~2.7×. A real investor seeking £5k/month would stop at ~12–14 properties.
  • The plans hold essentially no reserves. Every plan runs the cash account down to a minimum of £1,246 — across portfolios of up to £4m. There is no emergency fund, no capex reserve, no lender stress buffer. One boiler or one void breaks the plan.

Two of the three "strategies" are also nearly identical (Steady and Accelerated produce the same 30 properties, the same ~£4.09m, the same 27 purchases), so the investor is really being offered two distinct routes, not three. Several costs an underwriter and tax specialist would insist on — mortgage arrangement fees, repricing of fixed rates, lumpy capex (boiler/roof/EPC), arrears, director-loan tax (s455/DLA) — are not modelled at all.

What is already credible

True-cash modelling (deposits & costs leave the account); Scottish LBTT + ADS on purchase; monthly amortisation with a real interest/principal split; post-tax cashflow with an S24 vs corporation-tax toggle; CGT on disposal; liquidity-gated generation that will not spend cash it does not have; pathway ranking with a stated binding constraint. This is ahead of typical practitioner tooling.

2Overall Verdict

🟠

Significant Modelling Improvements Required

Sound engineering and an honest cash model, undermined by an optimism bias in the growth timing, a goal engine that overshoots rather than optimises, near-zero modelled reserves, and several missing institutional-grade costs and risks. Suitable today as an exploratory and educational tool; not yet suitable as the basis of a real capital-allocation decision.

Why not 🔴 “Not Suitable”: the core accounting is internally coherent and the cash model is honest — the defects are correctable assumptions and scope gaps, not a broken foundation. Why not 🟡 “Minor Improvements”: the growth-timing bias and goal-overshoot are not cosmetic — they change the headline numbers and the recommended action, which is the bar a committee cares about.

3Review Methodology

This was a code-and-output due diligence, not a black-box opinion. The board:

  1. Read the projection engine (server/services/scenarioEngine.ts), the strategy generator (pathwayGenerator.ts), the tax module (tax.ts) and the cost calculators (calculations.ts) to establish exactly how each number is produced.
  2. Extracted the engine's own stored projection results for the three live strategies (scenario ids 58/59/60) and recomputed independent checks (peak LTV, month the £5k target is actually met pre- and post-tax, event counts) directly from the month-by-month series.
  3. Tested each assumption against UK lending criteria, tax rules and 20+ years of portfolio experience, treating every default with scepticism.
  4. Graded findings by severity — Critical High Medium — where Critical means it would change the recommended decision.

All figures quoted below are the engine's outputs, reproduced to test internal consistency — not the board's own forecasts.

5Detailed Analysis of Each Strategy

5.1 Steady Growth  Risk: Medium-High

Strengths

  • Aggregate LTV peaks at only 48% — on paper, conservative leverage.
  • Reported DSCR floor 2.38× and zero DSCR breaches across 180 months.
  • Largest end equity (£2.46m) and income (£10.1k/mo post-tax) of the three.

Weaknesses

  • Overshoots the goal 2.7× — 30 properties / £13.4k pre-tax for a £5k target. Capital is committed long after the objective is met.
  • Reserve of £1,246 against ~£1.6m debt and 30 tenancies — operationally unrealistic.
  • Headline value/income inflated by the growth-timing bias (§6.1); the true figures are lower.
  • 27 acquisitions ≈ 1 completion every 6–7 months sustained for 15 years — execution-heavy.

Board view: the most "complete" plan and the one the engine recommends, but also the one most distorted by the growth-timing bias and the one demanding the most relentless execution. Attractive on screen, demanding in reality.

5.2 Accelerated Growth  Risk: Medium-High

Functionally a duplicate of Steady (see §4). In principle "Accelerated" should trade reserve buffer for speed and reach the goal sooner at higher risk; in practice it reaches £5k/mo in the same month (132) with the same 30 properties. The label promises a risk/speed trade-off the engine does not actually deliver. Until the templates diverge meaningfully, this should not be presented as a separate option.

5.3 Mortgage Recycler  Risk: Medium

Strengths

  • Lowest leverage (peak LTV 38%, ends with just £66k debt) and highest DSCR floor (3.63×) — the most resilient to rate shocks.
  • Simplest balance sheet: 11 properties, mostly unencumbered.
  • Still meets the income goal (£6.9k/mo post-tax) with far less debt.

Weaknesses

  • Capital-inefficient: ends 95% equity (£1.36m equity on £1.43m value). Paying mortgages down forgoes leveraged growth — the opposite of how experienced investors compound.
  • The name is a misnomer. Real "mortgage recycling" means refinancing to pull equity out and redeploy it (BRRR). This template pays debt down — it de-leverages. Mislabelling a de-gearing plan as "recycling" is a credibility risk.
  • Reaches the goal latest (month 149) and leaves the most growth on the table.

Board view: paradoxically the most defensible risk profile of the three despite the misleading name — but it sacrifices so much leverage that a sophisticated investor would question whether de-gearing to 95% equity is rational while the goal is "income", not "debt-free".

6Financial Logic & Internal Consistency

The accounting is mostly coherent — but three findings are material enough to change the headline numbers, and one changes the recommended action.

6.1 Growth is measured from the portfolio start, not the purchase date Critical

In the monthly loop, every property's value, rent and costs are scaled by a factor based on the absolute month index i from the projection's base date:

  • scenarioEngine.ts:285 — growthFactor = (1 + growthRate/100)^(i/12), then value = state.value × growthFactor
  • :304 — rentGrowthFactor = (1 + rentGrowthRate/100)^(i/12)
  • :306 — inflationFactor = (1 + inflationRate/100)^(i/12)

A property purchased in month 110 is therefore valued at £100,000 × 1.03^(110/12) ≈ £130,700 the instant it is bought, and its rent is set to £800 × 1.025^(110/12) × void ≈ £962 — even though the modelled purchase price was £100,000 and the modelled rent £800. The asset is marked up ~31% and the rent ~20% on day one, then continues to grow. Because the recommended plans buy 27 properties spread across 15 years, the £4.09m portfolio value, the £2.46m equity and the £13.4k/month income are all overstated. The correct treatment grows each property from its own acquisition month ((i − acquiredMonth)/12). This is the single most important finding: it biases every strategy optimistic, and most the acquisition-heavy ones.

Consequence

The goal may be reported as "reached" earlier and with fewer properties than reality. An investor acting on month-132 / 30-property figures could be making a decision on numbers inflated by a five-figure-per-asset margin.

6.2 The reported "DSCR" is not a lender's cover ratio High

scenarioEngine.ts:333 computes dscr = totalRent ÷ totalMonthlyMortgagePayment, using the full repayment instalment (capital + interest). A lender's BTL stress test is an Interest Coverage Ratio: rent ÷ (interest-only payment at a stressed rate, typically ~5.5% or pay-rate + 2%), required to clear 125% (basic-rate) or 145% (higher-rate / limited company). The engine's metric and a lender's metric are different animals; a healthy-looking 2.38× here does not mean the purchases would actually pass underwriting. The two should not share the label "DSCR".

6.3 Mortgage rates never reprice High

Each mortgage's monthly_mortgage is fixed at purchase and held for the full 15 years; the loop only amortises principal (:295–299). In reality BTL fixes run 2–5 years and then revert/refix at the prevailing rate. Holding 5.5% for 15 years across a £1.6m debt book is a large, unstated optimism — and it is the assumption a credit analyst would stress first. (The What-If tool offers a manual rate-shock, but the generated strategies contain no repricing events.)

6.4 What the engine gets right Confirmed sound

  • True cash model: on a purchase the deposit + LBTT + 8% ADS + £2,000 legal are deducted from the cash account (buy_property handler; calculations.ts cost calculators), and a mortgage payoff removes the cleared balance from cash (:253–254). Cash cannot be spent twice.
  • Amortisation uses a correct interest/principal split (:292–299) and the interest portion feeds the tax calc.
  • Post-tax cashflow (:330, tax.ts) applies S24 (rental profit × marginal rate − 20% interest credit) or corporation tax, and CGT on disposal — a genuine differentiator.
  • Liquidity gating: the generator only schedules a purchase when the post-tax cash account can fund deposit + costs, so plans never rely on money that isn't there (min cash stays positive at £1,246).

12Scenario-Engine Design Review

The engine is a clean, deterministic month-by-month simulator with a real cash account and an event model (buy, sell, remortgage, payoff, rate-change, rent-change, vacancy, director-loan, major-expense). For decision support, these gaps matter:

Missing event types

  • Per-property refurb / void-on-acquisition period
  • Fixed-rate expiry / auto-reprice (revert-to-rate)
  • Capex events (boiler, roof, EPC upgrade) on a schedule
  • Arrears / bad-debt shock distinct from voids
  • Equity-release refinance used as a deposit source (true BRRR)

Missing calculations

  • Lender ICR at a stress rate (125/145%)
  • Per-property acquisition-dated growth (fixes §6.1)
  • Reserve / liquidity-runway metric (months of cover)
  • Mortgage arrangement & exit (ERC) fees
  • Return metrics: IRR, money-on-money, ROCE, net yield on cost

Missing reports / KPIs

  • IRR / equity multiple / payback period per strategy
  • Debt maturity & refix calendar
  • Stressed-case cashflow (rate +2%, void 8%) as a standing output, not a manual toggle
  • Reserve adequacy & "months to insolvency under shock"

Missing validations / warnings

  • Warn when a buy would fail lender ICR
  • Warn when reserves fall below an N-month floor
  • Warn on concentration (area / unit-type)
  • Flag when a plan overshoots the goal (see §13)
  • Surface the growth-timing assumption explicitly

Missing scenario types worth adding: a downturn case (negative growth + higher voids + rate spike), an interest-only vs repayment comparison, and a stochastic / Monte-Carlo band around the single deterministic path so the user sees a range, not a false-precision point estimate.

13Goal-Engine Review — Does It Optimise?

The goal engine is genuinely innovative (cash-gated generation, post-tax solving, ranked pathways with a binding constraint). But on the evidence it does not yet produce optimal strategies for the stated goal.

13.1 It maximises, it doesn't optimise Critical

The goal is £5,000/month. The engine keeps buying whenever cash allows, all the way to the horizon, finishing at 30 properties and £13,412/month — ~2.7× the target. It treats "more" as "better" rather than solving for "reach £5k/month with the least capital, debt and risk, then stop." A human investor told "I want £5k/month" would not take on £1.6m of debt and 30 tenancies to get there. The engine answers a different question than the one asked.

13.2 The pathways are not meaningfully different High

Steady and Accelerated converge on the same plan (§4). True optionality would span a genuine frontier — e.g. fewest properties, lowest debt, fastest to goal, most resilient to a rate shock. Today the user gets two copies of one idea plus a de-gearing plan.

13.3 It optimises on flattering inputs High

Because ranking uses the same projection that carries the growth-timing bias and no rate repricing, the "recommended" pathway is chosen on optimistic numbers. Fix §6.1 and §6.3 and the ranking could change.

Would a human investor make the same choice?

No. A seasoned investor targeting £5k/month would buy ~12–14 leveraged units (interest-only), stop once the goal is met with a margin, hold a 6-month reserve, and keep debt working rather than paying it off. None of the three generated plans is that plan — the engine should be able to produce it.

15Final Verdict & Investment-Committee Decision

🟠

Significant Modelling Improvements Required

A well-engineered, honestly-accounted cash engine that is currently too optimistic and answers the wrong optimisation question to drive a six-figure decision.

Would the committee approve this engine for making real investment decisions?

Not yet — approved for exploration and education, declined for capital allocation.

The decision is finely balanced and deliberately not red. The foundations a committee cares about most — money that cannot be spent twice, real transaction taxes, genuine amortisation, post-tax cashflow — are present and correct, which is more than can be said for most landlord spreadsheets. But three things stop it being decision-grade: the projection flatters every later purchase (growth measured from the wrong date), the goal engine overshoots the objective ~2.7× instead of solving it, and the plans carry no reserves while ignoring rate repricing and lumpy costs. Each of these changes the headline figures or the recommended action — the precise bar at which a committee must say "fix this first."

Ranked highest-priority improvements (the four that unlock sign-off)

  1. Acquisition-dated growth — eliminate the optimism bias (P0, item 1).
  2. Solve-to-goal-and-stop — make the goal engine optimise, not maximise (P0, item 2).
  3. Mandatory cash reserve — model liquidity like a professional operates (P0, item 3).
  4. Lender ICR stress + rate repricing — make affordability and rate risk real (P0/P1, items 4–5).
